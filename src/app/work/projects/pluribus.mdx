## Overview

Pluribus is a research project on multi-agent language systems and the basis for the paper *"Don't Merge Carol: Cross-Inhibition and Protected Dissent in Multi-Agent Language Systems."* It asks a deceptively simple question: when multiple AI agents reason about a problem together, what should the coordination medium be — and how do you stop the group from collapsing into a single, amplified opinion?

## The Idea

The obvious failure mode for multi-agent systems is making the agents too similar. A hive of homogeneous agents does not give you diverse perspectives; it gives you one perspective, amplified. The project — named after Vince Gilligan's *Pluribus* — protects a permanent dissenter named **Carol**, whose voice is structurally difficult to erase regardless of how strongly the others agree.

## Two Phases

- **Phase 1 — Blackboard hive.** A text-level shared blackboard with *cross-inhibition*: agents challenge weak reasoning, with influence rising or falling based on the quality of their arguments. Carol cannot be silenced. The finding: adversarial deliberation helps most on judgment-heavy tasks rather than simple factual ones.
- **Phase 2 — Latent Resonance Loop.** Agents encode hidden states into a shared continuous buffer using Behaviourally-Aligned Prefix Codecs (BAPC), resolve conflicts with a TIES-inspired merge rule, and update the buffer through damped fixed-point iteration.

## A Result Worth Being Honest About

An early Phase 2 result that looked promising turned out to be largely an artifact of representation anisotropy — very high cosine similarity in upper transformer layers was preserving a shared background direction rather than useful task information. After isotropy calibration, the system produced collective latent states (at GPT-2 scale and across three 7B model families) that were consistently distinct from any individual agent's. On all five test prompts, the collective decoded to tokens absent from every individual agent's top-10 distribution, with mean Jensen-Shannon divergence of 0.08–0.13. Downstream usefulness was more limited, with the clearest advantage on prompts carrying genuine strategic tension.

The contribution is not a broad quality claim, but an empirical demonstration that protected dissent and latent-space conflict resolution can produce emergent shared representations in multi-agent language systems.
