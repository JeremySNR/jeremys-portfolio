## What it was

AI Steve was the first AI candidate ever to appear on a ballot paper. We built it at Neural Voice for Steve Endacott, who stood in Brighton Pavilion at the 2024 general election. Voters could talk to "AI Steve" through a voice avatar any time of day, ask it anything, and push their own ideas into its policy platform. The whole thing ran on our voice stack, and it could hold up to 10,000 conversations at once.

## How it worked

The idea was to let an AI do the listening at a scale no human MP ever could, while keeping a real person accountable for the actual job.

- Around 5,000 "creators" talked to AI Steve and floated policy ideas
- Around 5,000 "validators" rated those policies, and anything scoring above 50% made it onto the platform
- The validators, not the AI, controlled how AI Steve would vote
- The Electoral Commission was clear that if it won, Steve Endacott the human would sit as MP and do the legal work. The AI was the front door, not the lawmaker

## The reaction (people really didn't like it)

It got 179 votes, and Labour wrote it off as a gimmick, saying "effective representation requires human empathy and lived experience." Plenty of people thought it was the end of democracy as we know it. That's all fair, but here's the thing: it was a world first, it forced a real conversation about AI and accountability in politics, and it ended up on screens around the planet. For a four-week snap campaign running as an independent, getting the BBC, WIRED, NBC and Fox to argue about your idea is not a bad outcome.
